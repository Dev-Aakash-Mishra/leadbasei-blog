The Dark Side of 'We'll Get Back to You Soon' in Indian Businesses

May 03, 2026 · 5 min read

Two months ago, we were reviewing a B2B services company's pipeline in Pune. They had run paid campaigns for six weeks. Good budget. Decent targeting. The leads were coming in. We pulled the CRM data and found 74 inbound inquiries. Eleven had received a follow-up within 24 hours. The rest had a note in the status column that read some version of: will call back soon. Forty-three of those leads had already gone cold by the time anyone touched them. The team was confused about why conversions were low. The answer was sitting in their own CRM.

If your pipeline feels inconsistent, check one thing first: how many hours pass between a lead enquiring and your team making first contact. That number will tell you most of what you need to know.

Where the Lead Actually Dies

The phrase "we'll get back to you soon" feels polite. It is operationally fatal.

In Indian B2B sales environments, we observe the same pattern repeatedly. A prospect fills out a form, sends a WhatsApp message, or calls the front desk. Someone receives the notification. The intention to respond exists. But the response does not happen in the window that matters.

A lead contacted within five minutes is 21 times more likely to convert than one contacted after 30 minutes. Most Indian SMBs are calling back in 24 to 48 hours — sometimes longer. By that point, the prospect has either moved on mentally, spoken to a competitor, or simply lost the urgency that drove them to enquire in the first place.

The lead did not go cold because of price. It went cold because of silence.

Before You Fix the Follow-Up, Answer These

When a founder tells us their inbound leads are not converting, we ask four questions before touching anything else in their setup.

  • Do you know your current average time-to-first-contact — in hours, not in general terms like "same day"?
  • Is there a defined person or system responsible for the first touch, or does it fall to whoever is available?
  • What happens to leads that come in after 6 PM or on weekends — do they wait until Monday morning?
  • After the first contact attempt, is there a structured follow-up sequence, or does the lead sit until someone remembers it?

Most teams cannot answer the first question with a real number. That is the gap. You cannot fix a response time problem you have not measured.

The Structure That Actually Closes Inbound Leads

Fixing delayed follow-up is not a motivation problem. It is a systems problem. Individual sales reps are not lazy — they are handling multiple priorities with no clear protocol for what happens the moment a lead arrives.

First Touch Within 15 Minutes

The first contact does not need to be a full qualification call. It needs to be immediate acknowledgment — a WhatsApp message, an automated email, or a call attempt. The goal at this stage is simple: confirm you received them. That alone keeps the lead warm while a proper follow-up is prepared.

A Defined Owner for Every Lead

Unowned leads are dead leads. The moment a lead enters the system, it needs a named person or automated workflow assigned to it. "The team will handle it" means no one handles it. We have seen CRMs with 200 open leads and no assignment on any of them.

A Sequence, Not a Single Call

One follow-up attempt is not a follow-up strategy. The standard we work with: five to seven touch points spaced across seven to ten days — a mix of call attempts, WhatsApp messages, and email. Most Indian sales teams make one or two attempts and mark the lead as unresponsive. The lead was not unresponsive. The sequence was too short.

After-Hours Coverage

Over 40% of inbound leads are generated outside business hours. If your first contact only happens during 10 AM to 6 PM, Monday to Friday, you are structurally unreachable for nearly half your pipeline. An automated response system — even a simple WhatsApp bot that acknowledges the enquiry and sets expectations — recovers a significant share of that lost volume.

Build Internally or Deploy a System?

We are frequently asked whether Indian businesses should build their own response automation or use a configured system. The honest answer depends on one thing: whether you have someone whose job is to maintain and iterate the system after it is built.

Internal builds work when there is a dedicated ops or sales ops resource. Without that, internal builds degrade within six to eight weeks as exceptions pile up and no one updates the logic. The WhatsApp bot stops triggering. The CRM assignment rule breaks. Nobody notices until the pipeline is already thin.

At LeadBaseAI, when we set up lead response infrastructure for a client, the first thing we configure is not the AI — it is the escalation logic. Who gets notified when a lead has not been contacted in 30 minutes? What happens on a Sunday at 11 PM when a high-intent form fill comes in? Those decisions must be made before any automation runs. The tool executes the logic. The logic has to exist first.

What the First 45 Days Look Like

Setting realistic expectations matters. Here is what we consistently observe after implementing a structured lead response system in an Indian B2B context.

Week 1–2: Response time drops from 24–48 hours to under two hours. Not five minutes yet — the team is still adjusting to the new protocol. Some leads that were previously going cold are now being contacted while still warm.

Week 3–4: First contact rate improves. More leads are being reached. Conversion from first contact to qualified conversation begins to increase. The team starts to see that the volume of "unresponsive" leads was inflated — many of those leads simply needed to be called sooner.

Month 2: The sequence length increases as the team builds confidence in following up beyond the first attempt. Pipeline consistency improves. Revenue from inbound is no longer random — it follows a pattern. That predictability is what was missing, not the leads themselves.

The Actual Cost of "Soon"

The pipeline problem most Indian founders are trying to solve is not a lead generation problem. The leads are arriving. The cost is in what happens — or does not happen — in the 24 hours after they arrive.

Every hour of delay is a percentage point of conversion probability lost. The question is not whether your team is trying. The question is whether your system is built to respond before the lead moves on. Right now, for most Indian B2B businesses, it is not.
